A Mix of Real and Fantasy

Miyazaki's Authentic Stories

Miyazaki’s authenticity is not only present in his perspective but also in the stories of his films, where he applies the Shintōistic concept of being in harmony with the world to creating films that are in tune with his own experiences. Compared to Disney productions whose stories “are based on familiar fairy tales,” those in Miyazaki’s films are “either original stories or his own adaptations of fairly obscure works” (Talbot, 2005).

For example, drawing from the memory of picking up trash in the river and cleaning it up, Miyazaki applies it to Spirited Away, where Chihiro pulls trash out of a creature made of dirt. By using lived experiences, Miyazaki redefines art, something that doesn’t have to be abstract but closer to reality.

Real People in a Fantasy World

The belief of making realistic fantasy films encourages Miyazaki to also make his film universe sympathetic and characters human-like. Miyazaki’s films lack a “black-and-white moral universe” since he said, “I’m not a god who decides on what is good and bad,” because “We as humans make mistakes” (Talbot, 2005; Miyazaki qtd. in Mishan, 2021).

This concept of sympathy is seen in Howl’s Moving Castle, where “the youthful-appearing Witch,” despite transforming young Sophie into an old lady, is taken care of by her without complaint upon reconciliation (Mishan, 2021). According to Miyazaki, the characters are a mirror of the world, so having them live with their mistakes gives them the complexities of real people.
